d'Market Landing Page Strategy

  ---                                                        
1. Positioning

d'Market is a UK-founded digital marketplace built for the
realities of Sub-Saharan trade — connecting informal         traders with buyers through real-time discovery,
intelligent matching, and tools designed to grow alongside
them. It is not a charity project; it is a commercially
rigorous platform with deep roots in how African markets
actually work. The business creates value for traders, for
buyers, and for investors who understand that proximity to
underserved markets is a structural advantage.

  ---
2. Hero Headline Options

Option A — Market Opportunity Framing (Investor-leaning)
▎ Headline: Africa's Informal Economy. Finally,            
Infrastructure.                                              ▎ Subheadline: d'Market connects millions of local traders
with buyers in real time — building the digital layer that   Sub-Saharan commerce has always needed.                                                             
Option B — Community & Human Truth Framing (Trader-leaning)  ▎ Headline: Your Market. On Every Phone.                   
▎ Subheadline: Sell your goods, find your customers, and   
grow your business — without leaving your stall. d'Market  
brings local trade into the digital age.                                                             
Option C — Balanced / Premium Brand Framing (Best for      
fundraising)                                               
▎ Headline: Where Local Markets Become Global Commerce.
▎ Subheadline: d'Market is a mobile-first marketplace      
giving Sub-Saharan traders real-time tools to sell smarter,
reach further, and build trust — at scale.                                                             
Recommendation: Option C for the primary fundraising page.
It works simultaneously for both audiences without           pandering to either.

6. Trust-Building Strategy

The core principle: Never claim trust. Demonstrate it
through structure, specificity, and restraint.                
UK Legitimacy — Where and How:
- Company registration number in footer (non-negotiable)
- "London, UK" in team bios (organic, not performative)
- Tone and vocabulary: British English, measured phrasing,
  no hyperbole
- SEIS mention in credibility strip (this is a specific UK
  regulatory signal investors recognise immediately)
- .co.uk or .com domain with professional email (no Gmail)                   
  Credibility Signals — Placement Priority:
1. Team section (faces > everything else for trust)
2. Validation section (market research specificity — dates,   countries, methodology)                                     3. Credibility strip (factual badges, not superlatives)
4. Footer (legal anchor)                               
   Anti-Scam Design Principles:
- No countdown timers, no "act now" urgency
- No testimonials with no verifiable identity ("John S.,   
  CEO")
- No generic stock images — especially no suited           
  professionals in a generic office
- No promises of returns, passive income framing, or       
  "opportunity of a lifetime" copy
- No over-the-top stat claims without sourcing               - Generous whitespace signals confidence — scam sites are
  dense and aggressive
- Professional photography standards — well-lit, real        people, real places
- Navigation structure signals legitimacy (real companies  
  have real pages)
- Privacy policy and terms links visible — most fraudulent
  sites omit these                                          
  What builds trust subconsciously:
- The site loads fast (performance = credibility)
- The copy doesn't oversell                                  - The team section has real LinkedIn-linkable people
- The problem framing shows deep market knowledge, not     
  surface-level assumptions
- Contact information is available and real                                                                               ---                                                          7. CTA Strategy

8. Tone & Style Guidelines

Do:

- Write like a serious business, not a startup. Serious      businesses solve real problems. They don't "disrupt" — they   build.
- Use specific language. "600 million informal workers" is
  better than "millions of people."
- Let the market speak. Quotes from real traders carry more   weight than polished marketing copy.
- Write short sentences. Especially in headlines.
  Especially on mobile.                                        - Use "we" sparingly. When you do, make it count.
- Treat investors as intelligent. They'll reject anything  
  that reads like it's trying too hard.
- Design for mobile first. If it doesn't sing on a 375px
  screen, it doesn't exist.                                                    
  Avoid:

- "Revolutionise," "disrupt," "transform," "game-changer" —   every startup says these things; none of them mean
  anything
- "Empowering communities" — charity framing; undermines
  commercial credibility
- Passive voice — "traders will be connected to buyers" →
  "traders reach buyers directly"
- Jargon without explanation — "intelligent matching" must
  be followed by what that actually does
- Overpromising — the site should feel like a company with
  integrity, not a pitch deck
- Too many CTAs — every extra CTA dilutes all of them
- Heavy gradients, glow effects, dark mode defaults — these   read as "tech bro" or "crypto"
- Fake urgency — countdown timers, "limited spots," "early
  bird pricing"
- Long paragraphs — break everything into 2–3 sentence
  blocks at most

opportunity. Human stories support the business case rather   than driving the narrative.

  case first                                                   - Stats are larger and more prominent

  quotes
